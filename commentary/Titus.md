**Tit 1:1** ***Paul***. Authorship of the epistle to Titus is disputed by modern critical scholarship. To take the text at face value we must at least assume it was written after the events of Acts, since Paul’s leaving Titus in Crete is never mentioned in that narrative.

***Servant of God***. Literally, slave of God.

***Apostle of Jesus Christ***. Paul was directly commissioned by the Lord (on the Damascus road) just as the other apostles were, and in that sense he is a special messenger/ambassador of Christ. I personally find some difficulty with this since the apostles were originally twelve in number and meant to represent/judge the twelve tribes of Israel \[citation needed\]. The replacement of Judas with Matthias seems to have been an isolated event necessitated by Judas’s failure to uphold his office \[citation needed\]. Are the twelve apostles so in a technical sense and Paul only in a general sense (he does elsewhere refer to the other apostles as “super-apostles”)?

**Tit 1:2** ***God, who never lies***. This is not a mere academic assertion of the divine attribute of veracity, but Paul is hanging an argument on it. We have no hope of eternal life if God is not 100% truthful.

***Promised before the ages began***. To whom or with whom did God make this promise? This seems like compelling evidence of some sort of covenant God made with himself (or the persons of the Trinity made amongst themselves) in eternity past, commonly called the Covenant of Redemption.

**Tit 1:4** ***To Titus***. Titus is not mentioned in the narrative of Acts. We don’t know where Paul met Titus, but he speaks highly of him in his other epistles (2Co 2:13; 7:6, 13–14; 8:6, 16, 23; 12:18; Gal 2:1, 3; 2Ti 4:10).

***My true child***. Likely indicates Titus was led to saving faith by Paul himself.
